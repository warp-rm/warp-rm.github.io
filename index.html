WARP-RM
Warp-Augmented Relative Progress Reward Modeling for Long-Horizon Robot Manipulation

Anonymous Author(s)

Submitted to the 10th Conference on Robot Learning (CoRL 2026)

Paper Code Real-World Rollouts
TL;DR: WARP learns a fully self-supervised, dense, signed relative progress signal from raw demonstrations by training on time-warped playback. Reweighting behavior cloning with WARP-RM produces policies that fold T-shirts up to 18× faster in throughput than vanilla BC trained on the same data.

Abstract ▾(click to expand)

Scaling imitation learning for long-horizon robot manipulation requires large demonstration corpora, yet human teleoperation inevitably produces mixed-quality trajectories containing hesitations, local failures, and recoveries. Existing frame-level progress reward models rely on absolute temporal progress proxies that suffer from label noise, or require costly human annotations to define subtask boundaries. We propose WARP, a fully self-supervised algorithm for learning dense, signed relative progress magnitudes directly from successful demonstrations without human labels. We generate per-frame progress magnitude targets via time-warp augmentations of demonstrations (variable playback speeds and reversals) and train a WARP-RM model to predict them from images. We then introduce WARP-BC, which uses these progress magnitude estimates to reweight action chunks during behavior cloning to preferentially upweight high-advantage action chunks. On a physical, long-horizon T-shirt folding task, WARP-BC folds 19/20 T-shirts from a crumpled start with a mean time-to-completion of 118.8 s, compared to 2/20 and 199.0 s for vanilla behavior cloning trained on the same demonstration corpus, yielding up to an ~18× improvement in successful folding throughput, with failed trials counted at the full 240s timeout.

Method

Long-horizon teleoperated demonstrations contain a mix of decisive, productive motion and unproductive segments such as hesitations, failed grasps, and recoveries. Existing progress models supervise on absolute normalized time, which is noisy because identical normalized timesteps can correspond to very different task states across demonstrations.

WARP-RM is trained instead to predict relative progress: how fast, and in which direction, the task is advancing. This signal is generated for free by replaying successful demonstrations at non-uniform velocities (slower, faster, and in reverse) and asking a model to recover the playback speed from images alone.

Time-warp sampler

Figure 1 · Time-warp playback schedule

The sampler resamples a successful trajectory using a warped playback schedule. Playback speeds are drawn from a stationary AR(1) process in log-space — producing smooth, temporally correlated speed variations from slow-motion to fast-forward — and the direction is randomly inverted at a Poisson-sampled set of reversal points. Each sampled window of N=32 frames is paired with a per-frame progress label equal to the signed source-frame displacement from the window's first frame.

Key parameters: N = 32 frames α = 0.5 σ∞ = ln 2 λrev = 1

Operating in log-space makes a 2× speedup and a 0.5× slowdown equally likely. The Poisson-sampled reversals expose the model to negative progress (regression).

WARP-RM architecture

Figure 2 · WARP-RM model

A frozen DINOv3 ViT-B/16 encoder produces a 768-d feature for each frame. Each token is formed by concatenating the frame embedding with its temporal difference, projected to 768-d, and passed through a 12-layer bidirectional transformer encoder. The model emits, at each input frame, a softmax distribution over 30 cumulative-progress bins. Following Stop Regressing (Farebrother et al., 2024), this categorical formulation improves stability over direct scalar regression.

The per-frame velocity used by WARP-BC is the discrete temporal derivative of the predicted cumulative progress: v̂t = (N−1)·(ŷt − ŷt−1) .

v̂ ≈ 1 → expert pace · v̂ ≈ 0 → stalled progress · v̂ < 0 → regressing progress

WARP-BC: action-chunk reweighting

For each training chunk of horizon H=30 frames (1.0 s), let v̂end be the predicted velocity at the chunk's terminal frame. The reward-aligned BC weight is:

w = v̂end · 1{ v̂end > τ }

With τ = 1.0, only chunks ending in faster-than-expert forward progress are kept, and they are weighted continuously by their velocity. The final loss is the standard flow-matching BC loss weighted per-sample by w:

LBC = E(s,a)∼D [ w(s,a) · Lflow(πθ; s, a) ]
